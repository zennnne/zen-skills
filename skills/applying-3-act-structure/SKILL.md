# Applying Three-Act Structure

A cause-and-effect framework dividing any narrative into three proportional sections.

## The Three Acts at a Glance

| Act | Name | Proportion | Core Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Setup | ~25% | Establish world, protagonist, stakes — then disrupt it |
| 2 | Confrontation | ~50% | Escalate obstacles; protagonist tries and fails; stakes rise |
| 3 | Resolution | ~25% | Final confrontation; climax; denouement |

For a 100-page screenplay: Act 1 ≈ pp. 1–25, Act 2 ≈ pp. 25–75, Act 3 ≈ pp. 75–100.

---

## 6 Key Beats

These are the structural hinges — every beat must shift story direction:

### 1. Inciting Incident (early Act 1, ~10–15%)
The disruption that breaks the protagonist's "normal world." Forces them to respond. Without this, there's no story.

### 2. First Plot Point / Act 1 Break (~25%)
The "point of no return." Protagonist commits to pursuing their goal — can't go back to Act 1 life. This is the door into Act 2.

### 3. Midpoint (~50%)
A major crisis or revelation that reframes the protagonist's goal or raises stakes dramatically. Flips story direction; prevents "saggy middle."

### 4. Second Plot Point / Act 2 Break (~75%)
Everything seems lost. Protagonist is at their lowest. Forces a pivot from reactive to proactive — sets up final confrontation.

### 5. Climax (~90–95%)
The decisive moment. Main conflict resolves. Protagonist makes the final meaningful choice. Must grow organically from earlier events — no deus ex machina.

### 6. Denouement / Resolution (~95–100%)
Loose ends tied up. Changed state of protagonist shown. Theme reinforced.

---

## What Each Act Must Accomplish

### Act 1 — Setup
- Show "normal world" so the inciting incident has contrast
- Establish protagonist's desire (external goal) and need (internal flaw)
- Plant the rules of the story's world
- End with protagonist crossing a threshold (Plot Point 1)

### Act 2 — Confrontation
- Each scene creates a new problem or raises stakes — no static scenarios
- Use midpoint to prevent momentum loss
- Build toward protagonist's darkest moment at Act 2 break
- Subplots develop and complicate here

### Act 3 — Resolution
- Pre-climax: doubt, false defeat, or final obstacle
- Climax: protagonist makes an active, character-driven choice
- Denouement: shows the "new normal" after transformation

## Common Mistakes & Fixes

| Mistake | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Rushing the setup | Reader doesn't care about protagonist | Give Act 1 its full 25% |
| Saggy middle | Act 2 scenes feel repetitive | Add midpoint reversal; escalate every scene |
| Weak turning points | Beats don't shift story direction | Each plot point must change protagonist's situation irrevocably |
| Unearned ending | Climax feels sudden or forced | Plant seeds in Act 1; payoff must echo setup |

---

## Diagnostic Questions

To diagnose a broken story, check each question:

1. Is there a clear inciting incident that forces action?
2. Does Plot Point 1 commit the protagonist past the point of return?
3. Does the midpoint reframe or raise stakes significantly?
4. Is the Act 2 break the protagonist's lowest point?
5. Does the climax resolve from a protagonist choice, not luck?
6. Does the ending show transformation, not just cessation?

---

## When This Isn't Enough

Three-act structure works best for goal-driven plots. If it doesn't fit, default to **Hero's Journey** for myth-heavy or transformation-focused narratives. Use **Save the Cat** for granular commercial screenwriting. Use **Freytag's Pyramid** for classical tragedy only.
